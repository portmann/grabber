Fact-Checking Hillary Clinton’s Speech on the Economy

Here’s a closer look at the Democratic presidential nominee’s facts and figures

ENLARGE
Hillary Clinton spoke after touring Futuramic Tool & Engineering in Warren, Mich., on Thursday. Photo: Andrew Harnik/Associated Press
By
WSJ Staff
Aug 11, 2016 4:34 pm ET
27 COMMENTS

Democratic presidential nominee Hillary Clinton on Thursday gave an economic-policy address in Warren, Mich., that in many ways was a rebuke to the speech her Republican rival Donald Trump delivered earlier this week in Detroit. Mrs. Clinton’s speech included far fewer specific figures that could be checked. Most of her assertions were supported by economic data, but some were overreaches. Here’s a closer look at her prepared remarks.

Auto Industry

ENLARGE

“The auto industry just had its best year ever,” Mrs. Clinton said.

That is true, as measured by annual sales. American consumers purchased 17.5 million cars and trucks last year, according to Autodata, surpassing a peak set 15 years earlier.

Low gasoline prices stoked sales of pickups and SUVs and that helped Michigan-based auto makers over competitors that are more focused on cars.

In terms of jobs, auto- and part-manufacturing employment rose 4.3% in 2015 from 2014. That was the slowest rate of growth since 2010. Total employment in the industry is down by 4.8 million from its 2000 peak.

—Eric Morath

***

U.S. Workforce

“We have the most dynamic, productive workforce in the world,” Mrs. Clinton said.

Comparisons across international borders aren’t easy, but the U.S. does rank near the top among advanced economies in terms of overall worker productivity—not quite first, though. According to the Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development, the U.S. ranked third in 2014 for constant-price gross domestic product per hour worked, trailing Luxembourg and Norway.

When it comes to recent trends, however, the picture is grim. Productivity gains have slowed dramatically in the U.S. in recent years and productivity actually declined over the past three quarters, the longest streak of pullbacks since the end of the 1970s.  That could have serious long-term consequences for American economic growth and living standards. Economists also have expressed concern about declining labor-market dynamism and business startups.

It’s not just a U.S. phenomenon. Productivity growth has slowed in other advanced economies as well.

—Ben Leubsdorf

***

Economic Impact

“According to an independent analysis by a former economic advisor to Sen. John McCain, if you add up all of Trump’s ideas—from cutting taxes for the wealthy and corporations to starting a trade war with China to deporting millions of hard-working immigrants—the result would be a loss of 3.4 million jobs. By contrast, the same analyst found that with my plans, the economy would create more than 10 million new jobs,” Mrs. Clinton said.

She was referring to a pair of reports from Moody’s Analytics on the potential economic consequences of her policies and Mr. Trump’s policies. The report’s lead author, economist Mark Zandi, served as an occasional adviser to the 2008 presidential campaign of Sen. John McCain (R., Ariz.) and later provided analysis to support the Obama administration’s stimulus and mortgage-restructuring proposals. Mr. Zandi, a Democrat, has donated to Mrs. Clinton’s campaign.

While the report does conclude the economy would lose jobs under Mr. Trump, the lost jobs would largely be those of the immigrants he says that he intends to deport. The reports each include three different scenarios, one of which analyzes what the candidates would likely be able to actually accomplish in Washington; they conclude that neither candidate is likely to implement everything being discussed on the campaign.

—Josh Zumbrun

***

Trade

“I will stop any trade deal that kills jobs or holds down wages—including the Trans-Pacific Partnership. I oppose it now, I’ll oppose it after the election, and I’ll oppose it as president….I opposed the only multilateral trade deal that came before the Senate while I was there, because it didn’t meet my high bar,” Mrs. Clinton said.

Mrs. Clinton now opposes the TPP, but she didn’t always. In 2012, she called it the “gold standard” for trade deals.  Her campaign chairman said last month, “She is against it before the election and after the election,” a remark that came after Virginia Gov. Terry McAuliffe suggested she might be open to a shift in her position after Nov. 8.

While serving in the Senate, Mrs. Clinton opposed the multilateral Central American Free Trade Agreement. She did vote for bilateral trade deals with Australia and a number of other nations. Earlier this year, PolitiFact noted that she supported most of the trade agreements that came up during her time in the Senate.

—Ben Leubsdorf

***

Exports

“Right now, thousands of Michigan companies are exporting billions of dollars of products around the world,” Mrs. Clinton said.

The statement on its face is true, according to International Trade Administration data. The agency says Michigan exported $53.2 billion in merchandise last year, and nearly 15,000 companies in the state are exporters.

But it’s a stretch to imply the state is an unambiguous winner in global trade. Adjusted for inflation, Michigan goods exports have grown about 1% a year from 2000 to 2015. U.S. exports of goods rose nearly 4% a year, on average, during that period.

Michigan has lost 50,000 auto assembly jobs since 2000.

While that loss partially reflects increased automation and new factories in the South, some of those jobs have moved to Mexico, which has tripled auto production since the North American Free Trade Agreement took effect in 1994.

—Eric Morath

***

Stagnant Paychecks

“While corporate profits are at near-record highs, paychecks for most people have barely budged,” Mrs. Clinton said.

The idea that paychecks aren’t budging is popular among presidential candidates—Mr. Trump made a similar assertion earlier this week—and not entirely correct. When adjusting for inflation, median household income was $53,657 in 2014, down from a recent peak of $57,843 in 1999, according to Commerce Department data. But without inflation adjustments, incomes have increased by 32% during that time.

And more recent data suggests household incomes have accelerated, even though the 2015 household figure is not yet published. Annual wage growth in July matched the best reading in seven years, according to the Labor Department. Researchers at the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta found median wage growth has climbed from less than 2% in 2012 to 3.6% over the past year.

Meanwhile, after-tax corporate profits as a share of GDP hit a record high of 10.8% in the first quarter of 2012, according to the Commerce Department. Profits have grown more slowly than the economy overall since, and were 8.5% of GDP in the first quarter of 2016.

—Eric Morath

***

Rising Costs

“Incomes aren’t growing fast enough to keep up with costs like prescription drugs and child care,” Mrs. Clinton said.

That’s true for drugs, but not for child care. Disposable personal income rose 3.1% in June from a year earlier, according to the Commerce Department. Prescription drug prices rose 4.4% during the 12-month period, according the Labor Department. The cost of child care and nursery school rose 2.4%.

Child care is rising in price much faster than the overall inflation rate, which was 1% in June from a year earlier, but the most recent data shows the price of the service is growing more slowly than the overall measures of income and wages.

ENLARGE

—Eric Morath

***

Estate Tax

Mrs. Clinton rightfully noted Mr. Trump wants to eliminate the estate tax. “If you believe that he’s as wealthy as he says, that alone would save the Trump family $4 billion,” she said. “But it would do nothing for 99.8% of Americans.”

The $4 billion figure makes a lot of assumptions, including Mr. Trump’s net worth, his tax planning and investment acumen, and what’s in his will. According to the Internal Revenue Service, the estate tax is based on the fair market value of everything a person owns—such as cash, real estate or business interests—at the time of his or her death, less deductions such as mortgages or other debts, estate administration expenses or property passing to surviving spouses and charities.

The value of Mr. Trump’s estate, especially at some future date, is unknown. But he has said his net worth is around $10 billion.

If you assume it stays at that level, he doesn’t leave any to charity or a spouse, he doesn’t have any administrative expenses related to his estate, and the inflation-indexed exclusion doesn’t change, taxes would approach $4 billion.

Mrs. Clinton was right when she said eliminating the estate tax wouldn’t mean much for most Americans. Estate and gift taxes apply at a top rate of 40% above the $5.45 million per-person exclusion, which means the estate tax affects about 0.2% of those who die each year. In 2014, about 5,200 estates filed taxable returns, according to IRS data.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

***

Tax Compliance

Mrs. Clinton promised to cut red tape and simplify taxes for small businesses. “Right now the smallest businesses, the kind that my dad had—it was a really small business—spent 20 times more per employee to prepare their taxes compared to larger companies,” she said.

The Clinton campaign cited testimony from Martin Sullivan, chief economist at Tax Analysts, before Congress last year as its source on the claim.

Mr. Sullivan, who drew from an earlier IRS research paper, found that firms with more than 50 employees face an average tax burden of six hours per employee, versus 153 hours for firms with one to five employees. The burden falls sharply, to 46 hours, for companies with with six to 10 employees and continues to decline as firms get bigger.

Without vouching for the number of employees at Hugh Rodham’s drapery business, the claim generally checks out. If anything, Mrs. Clinton underestimated the relative disparity between the smallest companies and larger firms.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

***

Tax Loopholes

Mrs. Clinton also zeroed in on Mr. Trump’s latest tax proposal, which calls for a 15% top corporate tax, down from today’s 35% headline number.

“We do know that the 400 richest tax payers in America would get an average tax cut of more than $15 million a year from the Trump loophole,” Mrs. Clinton said.

The loophole comes from the assumption that so-called pass-through firms would reclassify themselves to take advantage of a new, lower corporate rate. Right now, these companies–including hedge funds, global accounting firms and wealthy law firms–pass on profits to owners’ individual returns rather than filing as a traditional corporation.

Mrs. Clinton’s math relies on an IRS report showing the 400 individual tax returns with the largest incomes and supposes their tax rate on pass-through income would tumble to 15% from the current top individual rate of 39.6%.

All that works out to about the $15 million Mrs. Clinton cited but it’s a theoretical exercise rather than an exact figure.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

 ***

Outsourcing

“Right now, when a corporation outsources jobs and production, it can write off the costs,” Mrs. Clinton said.

Corporations can generally write off many types of business expenses against their revenue when determining taxable income. Mrs. Clinton appears to be reprising an argument made by President Barack Obama in his 2012 State of the Union address, when he called out tax advantages of outsourcing. “The tax code currently allows companies moving operations overseas to deduct their moving expenses—and reduce their taxes in the United States as a result,” the White House said in a fact sheet. The president proposed denying those deductions and implementing at 20% tax credit for businesses moving jobs to the U.S.

—Eric Morath
