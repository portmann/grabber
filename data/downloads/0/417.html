Fact-Checking Donald Trump’s Speech on the Economy

Here’s a closer look at the Republican presidential nominee’s facts and figures

ENLARGE
Donald Trump spoke to the Detroit Economic Club in Michigan on Monday. Photo: Sean Proctor/Bloomberg
By
WSJ Staff
Aug 8, 2016 6:16 pm ET
145 COMMENTS

Republican presidential nominee Donald Trump on Monday delivered an economic-policy address in Detroit full of facts and figures about the state of the U.S. economy. His speech, which boasted 82 footnotes, generally presented accurate numbers but in many cases omitted important context and downplayed recent improvement. Here’s a closer look at his prepared remarks.

Growth

Tying Hillary Clinton to President Barack Obama’s economic record, Mr. Trump said, “Their policies produced 1.2% growth, the weakest so-called recovery since the Great Depression…and a doubling of the national debt during the Obama years.”

Mr. Trump’s figure significantly understates the pace of economic growth during the current expansion, which began in mid-2009. Average annual economic growth during the first seven years of the expansion was 2.1%, according to Commerce Department data. The economy grew at a 1.2% rate during the second quarter, a slight acceleration from the 0.8% pace of the first quarter.

It is correct that the average growth rate of the current expansion is dead last among periods of growth since at least 1949—as far back as quarterly records go.

What effect Mr. Obama’s policies had on that expansion is a matter of debate. Mr. Trump and many other Republicans say additional health-care and financial regulations caused businesses to become more cautious. Mr. Obama and his allies argue their policies—which include completing a bailout of the U.S. auto industry started under his predecessor and a financial stimulus program—prevented a deep recession from becoming a depression.

The strength of every economic recovery since 1980 has eased, suggesting a longer-run shift in the economy not tied to a single administration. Also, at seven years old, the expansion is among the longest since World War II.

The national debt, as a share of gross domestic product, has more than doubled since 2007 as Mr. Trump said, to around 75%, but net interest payments on the debt have actually declined. They fell to 1.25% of GDP last year, the lowest level since 1968.

—Eric Morath

***

The Labor Force

“There are now 94.3 million Americans outside the labor force. It was 80.5 million when President Obama took office, an increase of nearly 14 million people,” Mr. Trump said.

These figures are correct. Last fall, we took a deep dive into what we know about the millions of Americans who aren’t in the labor force, which is defined as Americans who are neither working, nor actively looking for work.

Over the past 15 years, the number of people outside the labor force has grown largely due to an increase in the number of students and retirees—and there’s good reason not to consider retirees as “unemployed.” Growth in the number of retirees, in particular, is not a mystery. A huge generation—known as the baby boomers—was born in the years following the end of World War II in 1945. Those boomers are now entering their 60s or even turning 70.

While school and retirement account for the majority of those outside the labor force, there are interesting trends in how the population outside the labor force is changing.

—Josh Zumbrun

***

Homeownership

Mr. Trump said “home ownership is at its lowest rate in 51 years.” That’s entirely true. The Census Bureau last month said the proportion of owner-occupied households fell to 62.9% in the second quarter of this year, the lowest figure since 1965.

What does that mean, though? The figures are partly a reflection of the housing bust. Younger households, meanwhile, may struggle to afford a home amid student debt, growing rents, rising home prices and limited inventories of starter homes.

But the Census numbers also showed a degree of economic strength: Renters are spurring a steady increase in overall household formation. Renter-occupied housing units jumped by 967,000 from the same period a year earlier. Overall, household formation has been fairly steady since the early days of the expansion.

A rising number of households suggests more people are optimistic enough to strike out on their own and helps further spur growth as they buy furniture, start families and move up the economic ladder.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

***

Food Stamps

“Nearly 12 million have been added to the food stamp rolls since President Obama took office,” Mr. Trump said.

This figure is roughly correct, though the trend of rising food-stamp use predated Mr. Obama’s inauguration and has reversed in recent years.

According to the Agriculture Department, roughly 32 million people were participating in the Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program when Mr. Obama took office in January 2009. As of May 2016, the latest available data, participation was about 11.5 million higher at 43.5 million. From fiscal year 2009 to fiscal year 2015, average participation rose by about 12.3 million.

The rise began under President George W. Bush, who pushed to broaden program participation and eligibility, and in large part reflects the devastating effects of the 2007-09 recession. The trend has reversed during Mr. Obama’s second term: Food-stamp use peaked in December 2012  and began to decline as the economy recovered and work requirements that were waived during the downturn have kicked in.

—Ben Leubsdorf

***

Poverty

“Another nearly seven million Americans were added to the ranks of those in poverty,” Mr. Trump said.

This number is right. According to the Census Bureau, the number of people living below the poverty line rose by 6.8 million between 2008, the year before Mr. Obama took office, and 2014, the latest available data.

Roughly 95% of the increase occurred in 2009 and 2010, at the end of the 2007-09 recession. The poverty rate peaked at 15.1% in 2010 and inched down to 14.8% as of 2014. The economic recovery hadn’t made much of a dent at that point  but there are signs that wage and income growth have picked up since then. New figures, including 2015 data, will be available on Sept. 13.

—Ben Leubsdorf

***

Who’s Working

“We have the lowest labor-force participation rates in four decades,” Mr. Trump said.

That was true, but isn’t anymore. The labor-force participation rate was 62.8% in June, and has been trending slightly upward since last September.

In September, however, it really did hit the lowest level in a generation. September’s 62.4% labor-force participation rate was the lowest since October 1977, almost 40 years. Labor-force participation hit its record high of 67.3% in early 2000, and has gradually declined since.

Many economists prefer to focus on the labor-force participation rate of people ages 25 to 54, the ages when labor-force participation is not as likely to be influenced by school or by retirement. Their labor-force participation rate of 81.2% has also been trending upward since September, when it had fallen to 80.6%. September’s 80.6% was the lowest labor-force participation rate since 1984 for prime workers. The economy accounted for part of this decline, as has a significant increase in the number of Americans who say they do not work because they are disabled or ill.

—Josh Zumbrun

***

Black Youth Employment

Mr. Trump said, “58 percent of African-American youth are either outside the labor force or not employed.”

The Labor Department counts about 5.9 million African Americans age 16 to 24, of whom about 2.4 million have jobs. That indeed means about 58% are outside the labor force or not employed, compared with about 49% of white youth. It’s true that black Americans are significantly more likely to be unemployed than whites.

In this case, however, the bigger reason black youth aren’t working is that they’re in school. More than 2 million blacks in this age group are enrolled.

—Josh Zumbrun

***

The ‘Real’ Unemployment Rate

“One in five American households do not have a single member in the labor force,” Mr. Trump said. “These are the real unemployment numbers—the 5% figure is one of the biggest hoaxes in modern politics.”

This figure is correct, but again ignores the issue of retirees. If you, your parents or your grandparents are retired after long and productive careers, they are part of Mr. Trump’s “real unemployment numbers.”

Among two-parent families with children under the age of 18, just 3.3% have neither parent working. That’s not to say there’s no reason for concern. One group in particular stands out as alarming: 29% of single mothers with children under the age of 18 do not work, about 2.5 million families. Single mothers without work account for most children growing up in families without a working parent.

—Josh Zumbrun

***

Household Income

“American households are earning more than $4,000 less today than they were sixteen years ago,” Mr. Trump said.

That’s basically right. Inflation adjustment is the key to this claim. Median household income, adjusted for inflation, was $53,657 in 2014, down from a recent peak of $57,843 in 1999, according to Commerce Department data. Without inflation adjustments, incomes have increased by about $13,000 or 32%, during that time frame.

More recent data suggests household incomes have accelerated, even though the 2015 figure is not yet published. Researchers at the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta found that median wage growth has climbed from less than 2% in 2012, to 3.6% over the past year. Economics firm Sentier Research estimates wage acceleration over the past year has returned real household incomes to pre-recession levels.

Mr. Trump’s statement reinforces the notion that many Americans don’t feel as if they’re better of than two decades ago. Mrs. Clinton, and her primary opponent Sen. Bernie Sanders, have also referenced similar statistics when voicing their support for an increase in the federal minimum wage.

—Eric Morath

***

Personal taxes

Mr. Trump said the average U.S. worker pays 31.5% of his or her wages to income and payroll taxes. “On top of that, state and local taxes consume another 10%,” he said.

Those figures are based on research from the Organization of Economic Cooperation and Development, which calculates the “tax wedge” for its 34 members.

Mr. Trump used a Tax Foundation analysis of those figures, which ended up being a tiny bit out of date. The latest OECD figures, for 2015,  put the wedge slightly higher at 31.7%. That ranks the U.S. 24th among advanced economies, well below places like Belgium, Austria and Germany but higher than Chile and New Zealand. Those figures measure taxes paid by the employer (such as the employer’s share of Social Security) and employee and are for an average single worker without children.

But the so-called tax wedge falls considerably when deductions start to come into play. For a married couple with one earner, two children and average wages ($50,964), it is 20.7%.

There are numerous other variables, of course, including wages, whether or not there is a second earner, the number of children and other potential deductions.

State and local tax burdens are another factor in determining disposable income. The Tax Foundation, which Mr. Trump cited as his source, put the U.S. average at 9.9% during the 2012 fiscal year.

The range varies from 12.7% in New York to 6.5% in Alaska, according to the group.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

***

Business taxes

Mr. Trump said the U.S. has the highest business-tax rate among major industrialized nations of the world. “It’s almost 40% when you add in taxes at the state level,” he said.

The OECD is again Mr. Trump’s source and the headline numbers are correct. But it’s not accurate to say all businesses pay those rates.

In 2012, profitable large corporations in the U.S. paid an average tax rate of 16.1% and about one-fifth paid no taxes at all, according to a Government Accountability Office report out earlier this year.

To be sure, there a multiple variables that affect the actual rate, leading to calculations—in the same report—showing companies paid 17.3%, or 18.5%, or 19.5%, or 21.1%, or 22% or 23% or 26.3% in taxes.  But most corporations have found a mix of legal loopholes and tax breaks such that they aren’t paying that headline 35% rate.

That also doesn’t change the fact that companies are often looking for legal ways to shave even more off their tax burden, leading some to leave the U.S. altogether.

—Jeffrey Sparshott

***

Nafta and Auto Workers

“Before Nafta went into effect, there were 285,000 auto workers in Michigan. Today, that number is only 160,000,” Mr. Trump said.

The figures accurately represent the decline in manufacturing workers employed in auto assembly and parts production in Michigan since the North American Free Trade Agreement took effect in 1994. However, the statement implies that all those jobs were lost over the border. That’s not entirely true.

Some of those jobs moved to other states. Alabama, for example, has added almost 13,000 auto assembly jobs alone since 1994. That largely reflects U.S. plants built by foreign-owned automakers, who tend to stay away from union strongholds like Michigan and Ohio. The auto industry also has become less labor intensive due to increased use of automation and robotics to build cars.

And since President Obama took office, the number of auto assembly workers in Michigan has increased, after falling sharply during the recession.

But the Mexican auto industry certainly does appear to be a large benefactor of Nafta. Auto production there has tripled since 1994. More than 80% of the cars built there are exported, most to the U.S., according to the Mexican Automotive Industry Association.

U.S. auto production fell about 5% from 1994 to 2014, according to the Transportation Department.

—Eric Morath
