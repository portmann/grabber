To Switch or Not to Switch? How Workers’ Career Choices Could Help the Economy

The smart choice for a worker may be at odds with what's good for everyone else, says a new report suggesting ways to bridge the gap

ENLARGE
In the U.S., a declining share of workers in agriculture and manufacturing over the past several decades has accompanied a rise in service jobs, such as in health care, education and financial services. Photo: Joe Raedle/Getty Images
By
Kate Davidson
  • Kate Davidson
    The Wall Street Journal
    CANCEL
  • BiographyKate Davidson
  • @KateDavidson
  • kate.davidson@wsj.com
Aug 11, 2016 7:05 am ET
14 COMMENTS

If you lost your job today, would you look to find another like it, even if you knew your industry was shrinking? Or would you cut your losses and shift to a new career?

The latter is a better choice for the economy and probably for you, researchers from Goldman Sachs’s Global Markets Institute say in a report released Thursday.

But convincing an employer to spend money training you in new skills that you can turn around and take to another company is a very tough sell. So is convincing you to give up your seniority, potentially move somewhere new and make a costly investment in yourself to start at the bottom in a new industry—especially if you are an older worker.

This conundrum is becoming more common for workers whose jobs are disappearing as more work is automated or outsourced, Goldman researchers argue. And it helps explain a persistent “jobs gap” between the types of jobs people want and the types of jobs that are available.

It also reflects a shift in people’s economic roles—from “doing” the work to “organizing, coordinating and supervising” the complex tasks behind it, the paper explains. This has led to a rise in what Goldman researchers call “adaptive occupations,” which require workers to interact more frequently with other people and exercise their own judgment.

Researchers say this type of shift—and the underlying conflict between technological progress and labor—is part of the natural evolution of the economy. In the beginning, many occupations are small-scale and innovative, but later become large-scale, standardized and more cost-effective. Wages go down, and workers are eventually displaced.

Although it benefits the economy in the long run by boosting productivity and paving the way for higher living standards, the researchers argue, it creates tough choices for workers.

“Often, the individual’s best economic alternative is to wait and see whether she can find employment that leverages her existing skills—rather than to invest in new employment possibilities—even if finding a new job in her current industry is highly unlikely,” the paper says.

They argue the decision whether or not to retrain is “a classic investment problem.” It requires heavy upfront costs, high risk and significant uncertainty. Given the choice between two uncertain outcomes, it makes economic sense to wait and see.

But if many people moved into more promising occupations, it would benefit the broader economy by generating higher income and more efficient allocation of capital and labor, which will support job creation, researchers argue.

What will it take to bridge the gap? The paper says policy makers must consider a range of solutions that focus on spreading the costs and risks of individuals changing careers. They include emphasizing different skills, such as critical thinking, creativity and interaction with others—what they call “foundational middle skills”—as well as reducing barriers to entry, such as occupational-licensing requirements.

They also advocate for expanding incentives for employers of all sizes to offer on-the-job training, including programs aimed at older workers, as well as for standardized labor contracts that would allow employees to commit to working for a company for a certain time in exchange for training. And they suggest allowing people to finance their own retraining by tapping into retirement accounts without penalty, or creating career-transition savings accounts.

“We believe that policy changes such as these are critical first steps to closing the jobs gap by better aligning what is economically rational for an individual with what is beneficial for the economy as whole,” they said.
