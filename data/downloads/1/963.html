Do Simone Biles and Michael Phelps Deserve a Tax Break?

An exclusion for medal-winners' bonuses vaults forward in Congress

Rio Olympics: Should There Be a Medal Tax?

Team USA Olympians such as Simone Biles and Michael Phelps get bonuses for each medal they win, and the IRS counts that as taxable income. But some lawmakers are hoping to change that. Photo: AP.
By
Richard Rubin
  • Richard Rubin
    The Wall Street Journal
    CANCEL
  • BiographyRichard Rubin
  • @RichardRubinDC
  • richard.rubin@wsj.com
Aug 15, 2016 5:00 pm ET
36 COMMENTS

A tax break for Olympic medalists is about to jump over another hurdle.

Bonuses paid by the U.S. Olympic Committee$25,000 for gold, $15,000 for silver and $10,000 for bronzewould be excluded from income under a proposal that’s moving forward in Congress.

For swimmer Michael Phelps, thats $140,000 off the top line of his tax return, with the actual savings depending on his tax bracket. For gymnast Simone Biles, its $85,000 and counting.

The bill sailed through the Senate in July, thanks to thesynchronized duoof Republican John Thune of South Dakota and Democrat Charles Schumer of New York. On Monday, House Republicans announced that theyll introduce their own version and advance it through the Ways and Means Committee in September.

Our Olympic and Paralympic athletes make numerous personal sacrifices while training to represent the United States on the global stage, Rep. Robert Dold (R., Ill.) said in a statement.But when they return home with a medal for Team USA, the IRS forces the athletes to pay a penalty for their success.

Tax policy purists are aghast. Income, they say, is income and should be taxed as income.

The U.S.tax code is in such bad shape in partbecause we have so many exclusions, deductions and credits like this one,” said Scott Greenberg of the conservative-leaning Tax Foundation.

Mr. Greenbergmay be no match for the midsummer Olympic moment. If the bill moves through the House of Representatives, theres one more turn before the homestretchthe presidents desk. The White House expressed support for the proposal in previous years, but Congress never quitestuckthe landing.
