Tax Planning Strategies for High Income Earners


Vanessa and I seem to change tax brackets each and every year. During our first year of marriage, we had little taxable income. As time has progressed, our income has increased and our tax situation has gotten more complicated. This isn’t unique to us. It’s just a part of life, and I know it happens to many readers as well.

There isn’t a whole lot we can do to control our income. It’s going to fluctuate each year, and I’ve learned to accept that. However, there are tax planning strategies that can be used to legally minimize the tax dollars paid to the IRS each year. You can implement these strategies yourself, or you can work with a qualified professional who can stay on top your situation and provide guidance.

In honor of tax season, I am going to write several posts related to tax planning strategies. This article will focus on considerations for those in a high income tax bracket, and a future article will focus on opportunities for those with less taxable income. If you find these articles helpful, or have suggestions for future articles, please share your thoughts and concerns with a comment below.

Strategies to Reduce Your Tax Liability

Maximize Retirement Contributions

This seems rather basic, but deferral of taxation is one of the most common and useful tax strategies for individuals who are currently in a high tax bracket, but anticipate being in a lower tax bracket at some point in the future when withdrawals (distributions) are taken.

If you spend less than you earn, consider contributing the maximum amount allowed within your tax-deferred retirement accounts at work (401k, 403b, 457, etc.). If you don’t exceed the income threshold set by the IRS, also consider contributing the maximum amount allowed to a Traditional IRA. These contributions reduce your taxable income dollar for dollar.

Tweak your Asset Location

Corporate bonds (or bond ETFs) and Real Estate Investment Trusts (REITs) are not tax efficient assets. The income from these investments is taxed as ordinary income, which means a possible Federal rate of 39.6%, plus State income taxes, and possibly the 3.8% medical surtax. That means high-income earners could end up keeping less than half of the interest earned after paying taxes.

Stocks are taxed differently, where income is a mixture of capital gains and dividends. Long-term capital gains (any gain from the sale of a stock held longer than 365 days) and qualified dividends are taxed at a preferential rate. That rate can be 0% (for those in or below the 15% Federal income tax bracket), 15% (for those in the 25-35% Federal brackets), or 20% (for those in the 39.6% Federal bracket). The 3.8% surtax still applies if applicable.

That means you should generally hold all taxable bonds and REITs inside of tax-sheltered accounts. Stocks and municipal bonds (see below) belong in taxable accounts. If you can’t fill your tax-sheltered accounts with bonds or REITs, put the highest dividend paying stocks in there.

If you own both actively and passively managed stock mutual funds (or ETFs), you should almost always place actively managed funds inside of tax-sheltered accounts. They are much less tax-efficient than passive funds. Or better yet, consider going completely passive because actively managed funds often underperform an appropriate passive benchmark.

Invest in Municipal Bonds

Some high income earners (singles with MAGI exceeding $200,000, and couples with MAGI exceeding $250,000) are now subject to the 3.8% Medicare surtax on all investment income. Municipal bonds avoid this additional tax, and typically avoid all Federal and State income taxes. That means the tax equivalent yield (the yield an investor would require from a taxable bond) has increased for those taxpayers, making muni-bonds more attractive.

Harvest Investment Losses

You can offset unlimited investment gains, and up to $3,000 of ordinary income each year by selling your investments that have lost value. If your losses exceed your gains and the $3,000 of ordinary income, you can carry them over to be used in future tax years (they never expire).

It can be difficult to keep track of all your investment losses throughout the year, but consider taking a closer look in November and December.

Consider Charitable Gifts

This strategy is only useful if you can itemize your tax deductions (most often due to mortgage interest deductions), and plan on making donations.

Appreciated assets are some of the most tax-efficient charitable donations. Donating these assets will allow you to avoid paying capital gains or the new Medicare surtax on the appreciation.

Let’s look at an example:

Suppose you have enough deductions to itemize this year, and you want to give $20,000 to a charity of your choice.

You can either donate $20,000 in cash, or donate 100 shares of stock worth $20,000 that you purchased 10 years ago for $2,000. Either way, the charity gets $20,000 in cash because they can sell the securities and collect the proceeds tax-free. Similarly, you will receive a $20,000 income tax deduction either way (assuming your donation is limited to 30% of your AGI or less).

If you donate the stock, you avoid $18,000 in capital gains. You could then use the $20,000 cash (that wasn’t donated) to repurchase the stock, and your new basis is $20,000 instead of $2,000.

Taxes are not a one-day-a-year event

Tax planning and preparation should not be done on the last possible day of the tax year. Many of the strategies discussed in the article are not possible at the last minute. For example, 401k contributions must be made throughout the year. You cannot lump sum in a contribution when filing your taxes. Small business owners need to be thinking about taxation issues throughout the year as well. For example, managing payroll, bookkeeping, income, and expenses. Planning in advance is crucial if you want to minimize your headaches and reduce the amount owed in taxes each year.
