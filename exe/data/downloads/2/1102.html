How Economic Anxieties Explain Donald Trump’s Appeal—And Where They Fall Short

A new Gallup analysis based on 87,000 interviews shows his supporters don’t face ‘abnormally high economic stress’

ENLARGE
Air Force veteran Nick Ferrante, 77, waits for a bus on Saturday in Windber, Pa., where the median household income is $23,261. Donald Trump has been holding frequent rallies in the state. A new analysis shows his appeal among supporters is more complicated than simple economic gauges. Photo: Mark Makela/Getty Images
By
Nick Timiraos
  • Nick Timiraos
    The Wall Street Journal
    CANCEL
  • BiographyNick Timiraos
  • @NickTimiraos
  • Google+
  • Nick.Timiraos@wsj.com
Aug 16, 2016 12:19 pm ET
530 COMMENTS

A new analysis finds supporters of Donald Trump can be identified more easily by measures of racial isolation and cultural anxiety than by measures of personal economic well-being.

The analysis, unlike some others, finds little evidence that the Republican presidential candidate has received stronger support in regions with greater exposure to trade competition and immigration. It also shows his appeal among supporters is more complicated than simple economic gauges and extends more broadly to fears their children will enjoy less opportunity than they did.

The study, conducted by Jonathan Rothwell, a senior economist at Gallup, is based on 87,000 interviews collected over the last year. It looks at how attitudes towards Mr. Trump’s supporters and nonsupporters change between individuals in different local economies.

The study has received a lot of attention because of how it’s been characterized by different media outlets. At Vox, Matthew Yglesias says the study shows that economic anxiety doesn’t explain any of Mr. Trump’s support, and Slate agrees. Others say this oversimplifies the relatively nuanced conclusion of the paper, including at New York magazine and Mother Jones.

Here are four notable findings:

First, the paper makes several important demographic observations. Mr. Trump’s supporters are older, with higher household incomes, and are more likely to be white non-Hispanic men. They are less likely to identify with the LGBTQ movement or have bachelor’s degrees. They are more likely to be veterans or related to them, more likely to work in blue-collar occupations, and more likely to be Christian and report that religion is important to them.

Second, Mr. Trump’s supporters don’t appear to be worse-off than nonsupporters by certain economic gauges. The data doesn’t indicate Mr. Trump’s supporters are facing “abnormally high economic stress” when measured by levels of employment and income. They are less likely to be unemployed and more likely to be self-employed. They have lower labor-force participation rates, but not when one accounts for the age distribution, reflecting a greater concentration of retirees. They also tend to earn more than people who don’t support Mr. Trump, though this partly reflects how Republicans, in general, are higher earners.

Unlike other analyses, it finds areas with higher shares of manufacturing employment—and therefore more exposure to trade competition—have no effect on support for Mr. Trump.

Third, people living in regions with poorer health outcomes and intergenerational mobility are more likely to support Mr. Trump. Compared with other Republican or Democratic candidates in the current election cycle, Mr. Trump is the only candidate where lower rates of intergenerational mobility predict greater support, Mr. Rothwell writes. This finding backs the idea that Mr. Trump’s supporters, even if they are better off than others, may be taken by “extreme political views when their health status and the well-being of their children fail to meet their expectations.”

Mr. Trump’s supporters are more likely to live in communities with higher mortality rates for middle-aged whites, and Mr. Rothwell says unpublished results also find people in regions with higher obesity rates and higher shares of people reporting fair or poor health are much more likely to back Mr. Trump.

Fourth, Mr. Trump’s supporters are “disproportionately living in racially and culturally isolated” communities. The paper finds stronger support for Mr. Trump in areas farther away from the Mexican border and in neighborhoods that are whiter and more segregated than their community as a whole. People in communities with disproportionately high shares of white residents are much more likely to view Mr. Trump favorably.

The study doesn’t dismiss the thesis that economic anxiety is behind Mr. Trump’s rise, but it does show that his strongest support can’t be explained by narrow economic self-interest alone. It concludes that Mr. Trump’s supporters can be identified more easily by cultural angst, which raises other questions about what’s to blame for falling attitudes of social well-being in these communities.

Many companies are clearly concerned about future market conditions. It’s hard to blame them, given the long-term structural changes in the global economy that have driven interest rates so low in the first place. The middle class is growing rapidly in emerging markets, and the developed markets are aging just as fast. Both trends have resulted in higher overall savings rates, the result of which is an environment characterized by slower economic growth and little to no inflation. Credit Suisse points out, however, that companies can manage the risk that investing money now will prove to be unprofitable in the future by spending in an incremental fashion – by carefully stepping up research and development expenditures, funding joint ventures in new markets, or making relatively small-scale acquisitions, for example. One possible reason corporate hurdle rates have remained static is that if companies reduce the level of returns they require from a given investment, they will eventually reduce their overall return on invested capital, which wouldn’t please their shareholders. But lower gross returns on invested capital don’t necessarily translate to lower profits. The Credit Suisse paper cautions that it is important for companies to judge potential investments on the spread between expected returns and how much it costs to borrow, rather than the ROIC number alone. -
