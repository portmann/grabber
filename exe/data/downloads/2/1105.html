    • Things to Watch on the U.S. Economic Calendar

    The U.S. economy has flashed mixed signals in recent weeks. Don’t expect a lot more clarity with the next batch of data, though minutes from the Federal Reserve’s July meeting and speeches from key officials should shed some light on the central bank’s thinking.

    12 Aug 2016 4:45pm
    By
    Jeffrey Sparshott
  • @jeffsparshott
  • Jeffrey.Sparshott@wsj.com
  • Biography
    •
    connect

    • 1 Minute by Minute

      Share on Twitter

      The Fed releases minutes from its July 26-27 meeting on Wednesday. Central bank officials opened the door to an interest-rate increase as early as September at the gathering. The door appeared to shut after a poor reading for gross domestic product and then crack open again after an upbeat July jobs report. The minutes should offer some details on officials’ concerns and causes for optimism.

    • 2 Listen Up

      Share on Twitter

      Atlanta Fed president Dennis Lockhart, St. Louis Fed president James Bullard and San Francisco Fed president John Williams are scheduled to talk about the economy this week. Messrs. Williams and Lockhart are viewed as closely aligned with Chairwoman Janet Yellen, who has emphasized plans to proceed cautiously in raising short-term interest rates, and tend to reflect the Fed’s policy consensus.

    • 3 Inflation Nation

      Share on Twitter

      Low oil prices, a strong dollar and slow economic growth have helped keep inflation well below the Fed’s 2% target. But the June consumer-price index suggested some firming for inflation, driven by rising costs for shelter, medical care, transportation services and clothing. Core CPI, which excludes food and energy, was up 2.3% from a year earlier. July’s numbers are due out Tuesday.

    • 4 If You Build It

      Share on Twitter

      The housing market has been a bright spot through most of the recovery, though it’s shown some signs of slowing in recent months. The National Association of Home Builders’s sentiment index for August, out Monday, and government data on housing starts in July may help show if the sector is holding firm.

    • 5 City of Industry

      Share on Twitter

      U.S. manufacturing activity has been expanding in recent months, though it’s been weighed down by the same global forces—oil prices, the dollar and weak global growth—as the broader U.S. economy. The Fed’s industrial-production report, out Tuesday, should help show whether factories have stabilized. The mining industry and utilities also are covered in the monthly index.
