What is Your Net Worth and Why Does it Matter?

Every financial decision ultimately takes you closer or further away from your goals. To evaluate your financial progress over time, one useful measure to consider is your net worth. Your net worth is ameasure of your overall financial health, and is calculated as the difference between your assets andliabilities:

Assets Liabilities = Net Worth

Anasset is anything that you own of value. This includes financial assets:

  • Cash (checking/savings accounts)
  • Stocks,Bonds, ETFs, Mutual funds, etc.
  • Retirement accounts (401k, IRA, 403b, 457, etc.)
  • Cars and automobiles
  • Jewelry and household goods that could be readily sold
  • The value of any business or real estate that you own.
  • The present value of pensions and social security benefits (if you are close to retirement).

Most people stop at financial assets when calculating net worth. For a comprehensive view, you should also include non-financial assets, such as your human capital (the present value of anticipated lifetime earnings). The reason for this is simple – human capital is converted into financial capital through the process of saving. For example, a recent college graduate often has negative financial assets, but significant human capital. When they begin their career, they are paid wages, converting their human capital into financial capital. At retirement, the opposite is true. Human capital is depleted, but financial capital should be peaking.

Financial liabilities include anything you have to pay back (any form of debt). This includes:

  • Mortgage
  • Student loans
  • Credit card debt
  • Auto loans
  • Money owed to relatives

A Quick Example

For the sake of simplicity, let’s assume the following for assets:

  • You have $20,000 in your savings account
  • $125,000 in your 401k
  • Your home is worth $150,000
  • Your two cars are worth $25,000

Total assets (ignoring human capital) = $320,000

For liabilities:

  • A mortgage balance of $100,000
  • Student loans totaling $50,000

Total liabilities = $150,000

Net Worth = $320,000 – $150,000 = $170,000

Why DoesNet WorthMatter?

Most financial decisions will increase or decrease your net worth. By tracking your net worth over time, you can determine if your financial decisions are helping or harming your financialprogress.If your net worth is continually moving upward, you are probably making smart financial decisions. If your net worth is decreasing, you might be making poor financial decisions.

For example:

  • Saving (spending less than you earn) increases your cash holdings and your net worth
  • Investing (usually) results in financialgains, increasing your net worth
  • Owning a home that appreciates in value increases your net worth
  • Buying a new car decreases your net worth, because new cars immediately depreciate
  • Buying consumer goods (usually) decreases your net worth, because they have very little value on the used market

Insurance is a tool used to protect your net worth.Most insurance plans will actually decrease your net worth over time, because you must continually pay premiums to the insurance company. But the small premium payments protect against major losses. Without insurance, an unforeseen event could destroy your net worth in a flash. So you trade small, continual premium payments for catastrophic financial protection.

Why Your Net Worth Can Be Misleading

Remember, your net worth is a financial snapshot at one point in time. Checking this number once is not very valuable, because it provides no context.

There are several situations which highlight this problem:

1) Your Stage of Life

  • Individuals just getting out of college often have a negative financial net worth, but significant earnings potential (human capital). They have no earnings, and have borrowed to finance an education or trade, but they expect to earn higher wages after graduating.
  • Individuals in peak earning years are able to increase their net worth quickly, converting their human capital into financial capital by spending less than they earn.
  • Individuals in retirement will often see their net worth decline with age. Instead of saving, retirees often dissave, spending their accumulated financial wealth.

These scenarios are neither good, nor bad. They just are. Your net worth will fluctuate throughout the various stages of life.

2) Stock market fluctuations

Consider someone who has $500,000 invested in the stock market. They might check their net worth one month and be impressed. But what if the stock market has a major correction like the one observed in 2008? Well, their net worth could be temporarily cut in half. But if that person stays invested long enough for the market to rebound, their net worth will recover. That’s why it’s important to track your net worth over time– to see how things have progressed over several years.

3) Comparing to others

Another major concern is comparing your net worth to someone else’s. Your financial goals are likely very different from theirs. You probably have a differentcareer, a different family situation, and many other differences. But your net worth doesn’t capture these differences.

For example, consider a net worth of $1 million. There are multiple interpretations of that number:

  • Some view $1 million as an unattainable fortune.
  • Othersview $1 million as an ideal retirement nest egg.
  • And for some, $1 million is not nearly enough. They desire morefor various reasons.

In the end, net worth is just a number. What matters is achieving your number. If your net worth allows youto achieve your goals, then you’ve made it.

How to Track Your Net Worth

There isn’t any perfect method for tracking your net worth, but I personally recommend the following:

  • An Excel spreadsheet
  • Personal Capital

An Excel spreadsheet is useful for several reasons. First, it forces due diligence. To list all your assets and liabilities, you must think about and acknowledge your financial situation. And second,it can help keep your family on the same page. Should something ever happen to me, my spouse or family could usethe Excel sheet to identify assets and liabilities.

Personal Capital is a much easier solution. After signing up for a free accountand securelylinking your financial accounts, the software automatically updates your net worth. It keeps track of your assets and liabilities in real time, providing a snapshot of your financial situation.

Conclusions

Your net worth isameasure of your overall financial health, and an important number to track. If you don’t yet know your net worth, start tracking it now, and keep track of the fluctuations over time.Justremember, don’t place too much emphasis on the number at one point in time, and be careful about comparing your net worth to someone else’s.

What matters is achieving your personal financial goals.
